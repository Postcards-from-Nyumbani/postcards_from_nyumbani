There's a playlist I've been meaning to share with you friends, it comes from the depth of my desire, despair and a turning point which saw me through a period of great angst and disappointments. The said playlist has a quirky name **I'm afraid she'z my type** with a peculiar description (all playlists need descriptions, offbeat ones to be precise) ==A UoN babe with a cam stole my heart, or what was left of it. This playlist on shuffle reminds me of my uni; it's me taking a stroll around on a cloudy day.== At the time of writing this description, I'd just attended a Huawei event at UoN and the camera woman was this pretty babe who left before the event could end, some Cinderella ass shit, worse because she left no clue behind, just a 6 second footage of me , which made part of their news. I'd initially began the playlist as an open letter to an infatuation but it quickly turned to an admission of longing, an admission that I wasn't happy and that i was deeply reminiscing my happy days, which make up the last bit of the description; *This playlist on shuffle reminds me of my uni; it's me taking a stroll around on a cloudy day* I was in the trenches and thinking about my university, damn i loved that place.

### The Spine

Every playlist has three or four songs that are load-bearing. Remove them and the whole structure collapses. These are mine.

**Jua Tua — M. Rumbi & Zawadi Mukami**

This is the opener because it earns the right to be. There is a softness to it that does not feel like weakness — it feels like someone who has decided to be honest. *Jua tua* — our sun. Possessive but generous. The whole playlist is trying to do what this song does in its first thirty seconds.

**Tamu — Zaituni**

The word means *sweet* in Swahili and the song delivers on that without being saccharine. I remember hearing this for the first time on a matatu and turning the volume up until the driver gave me a look. Some songs deserve to be louder than the situation calls for.

**Love Ballad No. 6 — Muthaka**

Muthaka appears three times on this playlist, which tells you something about the period. *Love Ballad No. 6* is the one I return to most. It has the quality of a letter that was written carefully and then folded and never sent — not because the feeling passed, but because putting it in an envelope felt like too final a gesture.

**happy ever after — Kinoti**

Kinoti is the most consistent presence here. Six tracks across the runtime. If this playlist has a composer, it is him. *happy ever after* is the one that plays when the walk home has gone on longer than necessary because you are not ready for the day to close yet. You know the feeling. You have taken the long route on purpose.

**Inemara — Winyo, Fancy Fingers & Lisa Oduor-Noah**

This one breaks the mood and earns it. Something older and rootsier arrives mid-playlist and it does not apologise. Lisa Oduor-Noah's voice has the texture of something kept carefully. It sounds like the Kenya that exists just outside the frame of the thing you are looking at.

### A Note on Listening to Kenyan Music in Kenya

There is a specific pleasure in hearing a song that was made thirty minutes from where you are sitting. The references are not metaphorical — they are literal. When a song mentions a matatu or a cloudy afternoon or the quality of light on a particular kind of day, you are not translating it through distance or cultural proximity. You are just *in it*.

This playlist is entirely Kenyan independent music and that is not an ideological statement — it is just what was true to the feeling. M. Rumbi, Zaituni, STACY KAMATU, Kinoti, Njoki Karu, Caleb Awiti, Two Tea, Baraza, Mutoriah — these are people making music in the same city, breathing the same jacaranda-heavy air, probably stuck in the same traffic.

There is something grounding about that. When everything else during that period felt unmoored — the uncertainty of final year, the question of what came after, the particular vertigo of being almost-done with something you had been building toward for years — these songs felt like they had their feet on the ground. Nairobi ground. I knew what that felt like under my own feet.

---

### The Back Half

The playlist does not end so much as it exhales. *Haunipati* by Two Tea arrives like a conversation you did not know you needed. *Hao ama Mimi* by Baraza and ach13ng' sits with the ambiguity its title suggests — *them or me* — without resolving it, because some questions are not meant to be resolved, only held. *Rudi* by Kinoti means *come back*, which is either a request or a memory, depending on the day.

*Walk Away* closes it. Also Kinoti. The circularity feels right — you begin somewhere warm and you end somewhere honest and the distance between them is the playlist.

---

I still see her sometimes. Not at UON anymore — we have both moved on from there in the various ways that final year forces you to move on. She still has the camera. I still have the playlist. We are both, I think, different versions of the people who existed in the frame of those cloudy afternoons.

The songs do not know that. They play exactly the same way.

That is what makes them worth keeping.
